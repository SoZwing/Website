Freedom, 2009
Exposition collective "Orientations", Cetinje, Monténégro
Crayon Graphite

Le Monténégro a acquis son indépendance officielle récemment, en 2006. Le pays réunit des zones qui appartenaient aux états limitrophes plus tôt dans l’histoire. Dès notre arrivée, mon attention s’est portée sur la langue parlée et l’alphabet qu’utilise ce pays. Car si la langue officielle est désormais le Monténégrin, le serbe, le croate, l’albanais et le bosniaque sont compris et acceptés. A l'écrit, les habitants du Monténégro utilisent autant l’alphabet cyrillique que latin, car les deux font partie de l’histoire du pays.

"želim da budem slobodan" 
"Je veux être libre"
C'est aussi le fameux "I want to break free" de Queen...
Dans un pays qui vient de gagner son indépendance, dans une école d'art qui occupe un espace qui ne lui appartient pas, et dans une culture où l’homosexualité n’est pas (encore) acceptée, "želim da budem slobodan" est une invitation à s’évader mais aussi à affirmer son identité, en tant que peuple, et individu.

Montenegro achieved independence recently, in 2006, and congregate areas that were parts of neighbouring countries before. When we arrived there, my attention has been caught by the language and the alphabet. The official language is Montenegrin, but the Serbian, Albanian, Croatian and Bosnian are core part of the oral culture. People of Montenegro use the Latin alphabet as much as the Cyrillic alphabet, both are part of their history.

"želim da budem slobodan" means "I want to break free", just like the famous song by Queen…
In this country that has just won his independence, in an art school that doesn't have its own place yet, and in a culture where homosexuality is not yet welcomed, "želim da budem slobodan" is an invitation to escape, to affirm its identity as a nation and as an individual.
